Blog 10: Companies 2: Electric Boogaloo

11 October 2021

Company Safari but with actually interesting companies this time.

I kind of messed up the last Company Safari thing - appearently we were supposed to write about companies we found interesting AND that we would be interested working/interning for. I got the first half correct in most regards, but the second was a little lacking. Here's an attempt to make that right.

Company 1: Google

Google has a lot of locations around the world - you can almost pick any country and find a Google office there. Amsterdam is the only location in the Netherlands with a Google office, and Japan only has two - both in Tokyo. If you can ignore the moral implications of helping a giant monopoly make inroads on collecting all the data in the world about people whether they want to be probed or not, Google is a good company to work for. Granted, it's largest locations are in California and the US in general, which always leaves the opportunity for getting dragged kicking and screaming back to America. Ugh. Google does more than just seach results - Google Docs, Phones, IT services through their cloud services (The word cloud has lost its meaning on me at this point), and more. There's no shortage of opportunities with Google.

Company 2: Ellips Produce Grading

Ellips is an interesting company to say the least. Operating out of a small office by IT standards in Eindhoven, they create software that checks fruit and produce in general for imperfections, grading them and determining which fruit have superficial imperfections, which fruit appear fine outside but have rotten insides, and other fruit logistics. They seem to be using a lot of Machine Learning to make their programs better, which means the coders don't actually understand what's going on inside the black box of their code. The company boasts a small team - probably under 50 people - and claims to have a good environment for coding. For once, I may actually believe that claim. Although the work seems daunting as I don't fully understand the innerworkings of Machine Learning, and the fact that they seem to be more interested in WO interns than HBOs, this one is a moderately good fit.

Company 3: ICT Group

ICT Group is a massive group doing ICT. You probably got that from the name of their company, but it's an apt description. Based in Rotterdam, they seem to have their fingers in all sorts of pies - Healthcare, manufacturing, logistics, oil & gas, and the one that caught my eye the most: rail. ICT Group seems to be somehow involved with Nederlandse Spoorwegen in the design and maintenance of their signalling systems, which may involve ERTMS and ETCS in some capacity since that's the new standard. As a train geek, this makes the company infinitely more interesting than the others since it seems to be right up my alley. Rotterdam is a great city too, with an awesome station that I was recently able to visit. They have internships available, however they seem to go fast as they're already filled for the upcoming semester. All in all, a boring name for an interesting company!

Company 4: CM.com

Here's another company with a racing connection: CM.com sponsors the Zandvoort Circuit, home to the Dutch Grand Prix from 2021 onwards. To the best of my understanding, CM makes costumer service tools and programs, with things like automated chat bots, payment systems, and those annoying automated voice robots that answer your calls just to annoy you, it seems. Despite not being the most interesting company in the world, they do seem to be a more modern company with a desire to have a decent working environment. Their main offices are in Breda, with smaller offices in Arnhem and Utrecht, and a few international offices too - but none in Japan, interstingly. A pretty straight forward company.

Company 5: Heusinkveld

If you thought some of the other companies were out of left field, this one is crazy. Niels Heusinkveld is a... well, I'm not sure what to call him. He's a Dutchman living in Groningen, that's for sure, but his creations that he's made in racing simulators like Automobilista make you think he's a physics god or something.When he's just messing around, he makes creations like a driveable shed, a giant 390 ton tonka truck, and some other wacky creations that make you question whether he should be left alone with a computer. Although at one point he was instrumental in the success of the Automobilista simulator, coding the exceptional physics of that particular program, he has since moved into the physical realm, making sim racing pedals. Really, really nice sim racing pedals. His company is growing at an alarming rate thanks to Youtube exposure and the general popularity of the sim racing market as a whole, and as such I could see him expanding his venture in the future. Granted, his company is very, very small so jobs would be more of a lottery than with a larger company, but at the same time soneone needs to code the firmware of the pedals.

Company 6: Reiza Studios

Another left field company, not even based in Europe or North America. Reiza studios are the makers of the aformentioned Automobilista game and its successor, Automobilista 2. Naturally, coders are required to make this game work, as they are required to make their website, which currently says its under construction and provides no information on careers at their company. My understanding is, like Heusinkveld, the company is tiny, and as such there's likely few opportunities to work for the company. Nonetheless, sim racing is one of my hobbies, and to be able to work on my simulator of choice would be awesome - and, as Niels Heusinkveld indirectly proved through his time working for them, there's no need to move to Brazil, where the company is based, so theoretically you could work from literally anywhere with an internet connection.
